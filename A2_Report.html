Dataset

Following the class website, this dataset includes “all residential sales transactions in the City of Boston between 2000-2023”. There is information is various information about units, current and past prices, buyers, and whether or not any party in the transaction intends to flip or has flipped the property. There are nearly 50000 entries in the dataset, though some denote the sale of multiple units in a single transaction so there are even more total unit sales.

  2. How can we quantify the value of “opportunity” (i.e. jobs, property taxes) for lower/middle class?

The motivation for the overarching questions comes from the Boston Globe Article and MAPC Report linked on the class website. Specifically, the motivations are:

  1. The Boston Globe article talks a lot about the fact that many luxury units in Boston sit empty most of the time but fails to provide reasons as to why. I would imagine that this is a problem in many major cities, but the article fails to contextualize it despite the prevalence of a national housing crisis.
  2. The argument that luxury properties or just generally luxury endeavors provide jobs is often given. It is specifically given in the Boston Globe article by developers of properties like 30 Dalton. I want to understand how much merit this holds.
  3. This is related to the first question – something mentioned in the Boston Globe article that is not further explained beyond this sentence. I would like to better understand how Boston got to be in this situation, as understanding the factors helps us to better figure out a solution.
  4. The Boston Globe Article repeatedly mentions how hard it is to get affordable housing and their interview with Denise Delgado helps dispel the myth that affordable housing is handed to you.   5. The MAPC report focuses on the impact of investors on the Boston housing market but only states that Boston is attractive to them because of “lack of supply and relatively inelastic demand for housing”. I would like to understand and contextualize exactly how inelastic demand is.
  6. The MAPC report helpfully lists policy recommendations and bills active in the current legislative session. However, I believe more could be said about which urban areas, especially given that our dataset only considers Boston, flipping is most prevalent in. Discoveries & Insights

Our analysis began with an exploration of the data, in order to assess the general shape of the data as well as quality issues.

Before attempting to create any visuals, the data was examined in its raw form to see if there were any glaring issues with its current form. From this early analysis, it was noticed the way units were denoted in street addresses could vary widely. Some addresses contained dashes either within the street address itself or after the address in the unit number. It was assumed that these types of addresses denoted a single unit or building. However, some entries as some would list street addresses that ended with unit numbers of the form “1&2”. It was assumed that these addresses were indicating the sale of multiple units. Data detailing the sale of multiple units was removed so that all entries represented the transaction of a single unit.

The above visual also features data from the 02190 zip code, which is indicated as being in Weymouth rather than the city of Boston. This indicates that there may be some issues in terms of the data having a consistent definition of what constitutes Boston. Given that the physical distance of the 02190 zip code from the others and its different city classification, it has been excluded from all subsequent analysis for clarity.

Once we had an overall sense of the data’s shape, we moved into answering the revised questions posed earlier.

Summary

From this analysis, we see that property flipping is a recent phenomenon in Boston, with the problem getting worse after the 2008 Financial Crisis despite relatively inelastic demand for housing. Moreover, we can conclude that location very much affects how likely a property is to be flipped, with more expensive locations having a higher percentage of flips compared to other areas. It appears factors besides building type and amenities of a property affect a building’s chances of being flipped, as location and cost are the only stand out factors here affecting changes of a purchase resulting a flip. A dataset containing more information about individual properties would be interesting to examine, as it might lend more insight as to what factors affect a building’s cost and how properties vary across zip codes.
